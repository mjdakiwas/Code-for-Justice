What is Digital Accessibility?

Digital Accessibility is the design of technology products and environments to help people with various disabilities not to be impeded or otherwise unable to partake in use of the service, product or function.

It ensures that websites, applications, and digital tools are usable by everyone, regardless of their abilities. This includes people with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive disabilities. Accessible design removes barriers, enabling all users to perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with digital content effectively.

Why it Matters?

Accessibility is not just a legal requiremnt or a checklist item, it's a commitment to inclusion. Over one billion people globally live with a disability, and when digital experiences aren't built with them in mind, they're excluded from vital information, services, and opportunities.

By prioritzing accessibility, we create equitable experiences and open the door to innovation that benefits everyone.

Who Benefits from Accessibility?

Everyone can benefit from digital accessibility. Accessibiltity features like closed captions, voice commands, text resizing, and keyboard navigation improve usability for all users. Whether you're navigating a site in bright sunlight or using a slow internet connection, accessible design makes digital experiences smoother, faster, and more intuitive.

Tips & Tricks.

  1. Use Clear, Simple Language

    Write in a way that's easy to read and understand. Avoid jargon or overly complex sentence structures, especially on high-traffic or instructional pages.

  2. Add Alt Texts to All Images

    Ensure all meaningful images have desciptive alternative (alt) text so screen reader users can understand the content and purpose of visuals.

  3. Ensure Good Color Contrast

    Use high-contrast color combinations between text and background. This helps users with low vision or color blindness read your content with ease.

  4. Make All Content Keyboard Accessible

    Ensure that every part of your website or app can be navigated using a keyboard alone. This is essential for users who cannot use a mouse.

  5. Use Headings Properly

    Structure content using proper heading levels (e.g., H1 for main titles, H2 for subheadings). This helps screen reader users navigate more efficiently.

  6. Provide Captions and Transcripts

    Include closed captions for videos and transcripts for audio content to make multimedia accessible to users who are deaf or hard of hearing.

  7. Avoid Using Color Alone to Convey Meaning

    If you're using color to show status (like red for errors), pair it with icons or text to ensure the information is accessible to colorblind users.

  8. Label Form Fields Clearly

    All form fields should have visible, descriptive labels. Use ARIA labels or label tags to ensure screen readers can interpret them accurately.

  9. Design Responsive, Scalable Layouts

    Make sure your site or app works well on all screen sizes and allows text resizing without breaking the layout.

  10. Test with Real Users and Assistive Tech

    Incorporate feedback from people with disabilities and test with tools like screen readers, voice control software, and browser zoom.
